  ECE442 Project – Markdown Summary


  ## ECE442 – Network Science Analytics 
  Project Guidelines
  ### 1. Overview
  - **Type of work**: Research-style project
   on a **specific topic in network 
  science**, with an **in-depth study** (not
   just a shallow summary of a few papers).
  - **Expected activities**:
    - Survey of literature (papers, books, 
  tutorials, slides).
    - Analysis and/or numerical simulations 
  on **network datasets**.
    - Clear explanation of **main ideas, 
  methods, and findings**.
  - **Ambition level**:
    - Baseline: solid survey + meaningful 
  experiments/analysis.
    - Optional: original research that could
   lead to a **publishable** result (not 
  required).
  ### 2. Allowed Project Styles
  - **Experimental projects**:
    - Implement or adapt algorithms/models.
    - Evaluate them on interesting real or 
  synthetic network data.
    - Use existing software or write your 
  own code.
  - **Analytical/theoretical projects**:
    - Study a model, algorithm, or network 
  property.
    - Derive **rigorous theoretical 
  results**.
  - **Other possible directions**:
    - Scalable/efficient implementations for
   large-scale graphs.
    - Collect your own data from web / 
  social media and analyze it.
  ### 3. Teaming
  - **Strongly encouraged** to work **in 
  pairs**.
  - Working **alone** is allowed.
  ### 4. Choosing a Topic
  - **Goal**: Pick a topic you like, related
   to **network science**.
  - **Sources of ideas**:
    - Your own or your research supervisor’s
   projects.
    - Course links/resources page.
  - **Instructor support**:
    - Discuss ideas during office hours.
    - Get help with:
      - Refining the problem.
      - Finding datasets and code.
      - Identifying relevant literature.
  ### 5. Example Topic Areas (high-level)
  Some broad areas you can draw inspiration 
  from:
  - **Signal processing on graphs / graph 
  spectral methods**
  - **Graph anomaly detection**
  - **Network analysis of the brain / brain 
  graphs**
  - **Deep learning on graphs / geometric 
  deep learning**
  - **Tensor methods for dynamic/large-scale
   graph mining**
  - **Community detection in networks**
  - **Diffusion, cascades, influence 
  maximization**
  - **Compressed sensing / low-rank methods 
  in network analytics**
  - **Graph representation learning / node &
   graph embeddings**
  - **Graph neural networks for inverse 
  problems (community detection, link
  prediction, recommendation, etc.)**
  - **Graph generative models (VAEs, GANs, 
  autoregressive, diffusion models)**
  - **Fairness and bias mitigation in 
  graph-based ML**
  (Each of these has multiple 
  tutorial/survey references in the original
   page.)

  ### 7. Grading Breakdown
  - **Total project weight**: **70%** of 
  final course grade.
  - **Components**:
    - Proposal: **10%**
    - Progress report: **10%**
    - Final report + video presentation: 
  **50%**
  - **Extra credit**:
    - **+5%** if reports and slides are 
  prepared using **LaTeX** and the provided 
  templates.
  ### 8. Evaluation Criteria
  - **Technical quality**:
    - Are the models/algorithms reasonable?
    - Are assumptions sensible?
    - Are the conclusions thoughtful and 
  insightful?
  - **Significance**:
    - Is the problem interesting and timely?
    - Is the work potentially 
  impactful/useful?
  - **Clarity of presentation**:
    - How clearly are ideas and results 
  communicated:
      - **In writing** (final report).
      - **In the video presentation** 
  (structure, visuals, narration).
  ### 9. LaTeX & Templates
  - **LaTeX is encouraged** (not mandatory) 
  and rewarded with **extra credit**.
  - **Templates**:
    - **Report**: NeurIPS-style LaTeX 
  template (zip + example PDF).
    - **Slides**: LaTeX template used by the
   instructor (zip + example PDF).
  - If you use LaTeX, you **should adopt 
  these templates**.
  ### 10. Proposal Guidelines (≤ 5 pages)
  The **project proposal** should clearly 
  state what you plan to do:
  1. **Problem description**:
     - What specific problem are you 
  addressing?
  2. **Planned approach**:
     - Models, algorithms, techniques you 
  might use.
  3. **Initial references**:
     - Core papers/tutorials you will rely 
  on.
  4. **Software/tools**:
     - Libraries, packages, or languages you
   will use.
  5. **Datasets**:
     - Which network dataset(s) you plan to 
  work with.
  6. **Expected outcomes & success 
  criteria**:
     - What you aim to produce.
     - How you will measure success.
  7. **Additional notes**:
     - Anything else the instructor should 
  know to judge feasibility and scope.
  ### 11. Progress Report Guidelines (≤ 8 
  pages)
  The **progress report** should look like a
   **first (incomplete) draft** of your 
  final report:
  1. **Introduction & literature review**:
     - Clearly state the finalized problem 
  and summarize relevant prior work.
  2. **Data collection (if applicable)**:
     - If you built a network from raw data,
   describe the process.
  3. **Methods**:
     - Mathematical derivations.
     - Model descriptions.
     - Algorithms used/adapted/developed.
  4. **Preliminary results**:
     - Summary of results obtained so far 
  and datasets analyzed.
  5. **Complications & workarounds**:
     - Unexpected issues encountered and how
   you addressed them.
  6. **Work still to do**:
     - Remaining tasks.
     - What may be infeasible and why.
  ### 12. Final Report Guidelines (≤ 12 
  pages)
  The **final report** expands on the 
  progress report and presents completed
  work:
  1. **Introduction & problem statement**:
     - Motivation.
     - Clear finalized problem statement.
     - Literature review / related work.
  2. **Data**:
     - How you constructed or obtained your 
  network(s), if applicable.
  3. **Methods**:
     - Detailed models, derivations, and 
  algorithms.
  4. **Experiments & results**:
     - Experimental setup.
     - Results (tables, figures, etc.).
     - Discussion and interpretation.
  5. **Conclusions & future work**:
     - What goals were achieved.
     - What you learned.
     - Possible extensions or future 
  directions.
  6. **References**:
     - Complete list of relevant references.
  ### 13. Video Presentation Guidelines
  - **Format**:
    - Recorded video (MP4).
    - Short webcam intro (optional), then 
  **voice-over slides**.
  - **Length**:
    - **Maximum 15 minutes**.
  - **Group work**:
    - In groups of two, **both students 
  should present roughly half** of the 
  material.
  - **Suggested structure**:
    1. Motivation and succinct problem 
  description.
    2. Positioning your work within existing
   literature.
    3. High-level description of methods, 
  algorithms, and data.
    4. Presentation of key results.
    5. Conclusions and possible future 
  directions.
